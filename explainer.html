💡 The Hypothesis & Motivation

Every three months, the U.S. government publishes an official estimate of how fast the economy grew. This number — GDP growth — is one of the most watched economic statistics in the world. The problem is that it arrives about six weeks after the quarter ends, and the first estimate is still subject to revision for years afterward.

🎯 The Central Question

Monthly economic indicators — factory output, job numbers, retail sales, stock prices — stream in throughout the quarter as it happens. Can we use these early signals to predict the GDP number before the official estimate is released? And if so, how much more accurate are these predictions compared to simply extrapolating past GDP patterns?

This is called "nowcasting" — a portmanteau of "now" and "forecasting." The term originated in weather science and was adopted by central banks (the Fed, ECB, Bank of England) who need real-time economic intelligence before official statistics are published.

This project tests that hypothesis rigorously. We built and compared five different forecasting approaches, evaluated them on 20 years of out-of-sample data, and quantified which methods work, by how much, and at what level of statistical confidence.

⚡ Why This Is Hard

A few things make GDP nowcasting genuinely challenging — and these are problems the project had to solve explicitly:

1

The Timing Mismatch

GDP is quarterly. Monthly indicators arrive throughout the quarter. A forecast made in early January uses different data than one made in late March, even though both are predicting the same Q1 GDP number. This "mixed frequency" problem requires special methods.

2

The Publication Delay

Data doesn't arrive the moment the reference month ends. Payrolls come out ~5 days after month-end. Factory output takes ~17 days. Durable goods takes ~28 days. We simulate exactly which data would have been available to a real forecaster on any given date.

3

Too Few Data Points

We only have ~60 "normal" (pre-COVID) quarters to evaluate on. That's not many data points, which means statistical tests have limited power — it's genuinely hard to prove one method is better than another with high confidence.

4

The COVID Outlier

The 2020Q2 GDP collapse (−31.6% annualized) is so extreme it distorts every metric. No model trained on normal economic data could have predicted a pandemic. We report results three ways: including COVID, excluding it, and pre-COVID only.

⚠️ The "Ragged Edge" Problem

At any given point in time, the most recent data look like a table with ragged right edges — some months have all 12 series published, the most recent months are partially filled in, and the current month is mostly empty. Every model in this project was evaluated using only the data that would have been available on the forecast date — never anything from the future.

📊 The Data

All data comes from FRED (Federal Reserve Economic Data), a free public database maintained by the St. Louis Fed.

The Target — What We're Predicting

GDPC1 — Real GDP (chained 2017 dollars, seasonally adjusted). Transformed to: 400 × log(GDP_t / GDP_{t-1}) — annualized quarterly percent growth. Over our sample (1990–2026), this averages about 2.4% with a standard deviation of 4.4%.

📐 Why "Transform" the Data?

Raw levels (e.g., industrial production at 103.2) have trends over time. Regression models need stationary series — ones that don't systematically drift. Log-differences give approximate percentage changes (e.g., +0.3% month-on-month). First differences remove the trend from rates and spreads. After transformation, all 12 series are stationary and ready for modelling.

📅 Day-by-Day: What We Built

The project followed a strict 12-day build plan, with working committed code at the end of each day. Here's what each day produced:

1
Day 1 Project Scaffolding
Set up the code structure, installed all dependencies, created a central config.py that stores all settings in one place (series IDs, publication lags, date ranges, file paths), and initialized git. Nothing fancy — just making sure every subsequent day has a clean, organized place to put things.
2
Day 2 Getting the Data
Built a data fetcher that downloads all 13 FRED series and saves them to disk as parquet files. Key feature: once downloaded, re-running takes ~1 second from cache rather than hitting the internet. Daily series were averaged to monthly; weekly claims were summed. One discovery: FRED's SP500 series only goes back to 2016 — replaced with NASDAQCOM (full history from 1990).
3
Day 3 The Ragged Edge — Core Infrastructure
Built the publication-lag masker: takes any "as-of" date and sets cells to missing where the underlying release wouldn't have happened yet. Also applied stationarity transforms to all series. Defined the three evaluation vintages:
  • Vintage 1 (V1) — as of the last day of month 1 of the quarter
  • Vintage 2 (V2) — as of the last day of month 2
  • Vintage 3 (V3) — as of the last day of month 3
Written with 5 unit tests verifying the masker works correctly.
4
Day 4 Benchmarks — The Bar to Beat
Built the AR(p) autoregression (uses only past GDP; lag length auto-selected by BIC) and Random Walk (predicts the historical mean). Both evaluated using an expanding-window backtest starting in 2005Q1 — faithfully mimicking a real forecaster who never looks at future data.
📊 AR(p) pre-COVID RMSE = 2.47% — this is the bar every other model must clear
5
Day 5 Bridge Equations
For each of the 12 monthly indicators: aggregate to quarterly frequency, regress GDP growth on that indicator + last quarter's GDP, predict. Combine all 12 forecasts by simple averaging. If only 1–2 months of the quarter are available, use whatever's there (partial aggregation).
📊 Bridge combination V3 RMSE = 1.87% — beats AR by 24%
6
Day 6 MIDAS Regression
Instead of collapsing three monthly observations into one quarterly average, MIDAS keeps all three as separate features. This gives the model more granularity — it can learn that month 2's payrolls carry more signal than month 1's. Unreleased months filled with last-known value (carry-forward).
📊 MIDAS combination V1 RMSE = 2.11% — beats bridge at the earliest vintage
7
Day 7 Regularized Regression (Lasso & ElasticNet)
Combines all 12 indicators with 3 monthly lags each into a single 37-feature model. With ~60–80 training quarters, ordinary regression would overfit. Lasso forces most coefficients to zero (automatic variable selection). ElasticNet handles correlated predictors. Hyperparameters tuned by time-series cross-validation (no future peeking). Logged which variables were selected: INDPRO, PAYEMS, ICSA, NASDAQ selected in ≥95% of quarters.
📊 ElasticNet V3 RMSE = 1.90% — competitive with bridge
8
Day 8 Dynamic Factor Model (DFM)
The most sophisticated model: extracts one hidden "economic activity" factor from all 12 indicators simultaneously using a Kalman filter (originally developed for rocket guidance systems). Handles missing data natively — no imputation needed. But with only 12 indicators and publication lags meaning most month-3 data is still missing at quarter-end, the model can't leverage its theoretical advantages.
⚠️ DFM V3 RMSE = 2.77% — unexpectedly worse than the AR baseline
9
Day 9 Rigorous Statistical Evaluation
Implemented the Diebold-Mariano test — a formal statistical test for whether one model is significantly better than another. Results reported for three sample windows: full OOS, pre-COVID, and COVID-excluded. Also tested COVID dummy variables in the regularized regression models.
10
Day 10 Publication Figures
Generated four publication-quality figures: RMSE comparison bar chart, time-series of GDP vs. nowcasts (with COVID shading), RMSE-vs-vintage "information arrival" chart, and Lasso variable selection stability map. All saved as PNG (300 DPI) and PDF.
11
Day 11 Research Report & Documentation
Wrote the full technical research report documenting methodology, results, and limitations. Updated README with complete reproduction instructions (clone → install → set API key → run pipeline in order).
12
Day 12 Combination Forecast & Final Polish
Built a method-family combination forecast — a simple equal-weight average of Bridge, MIDAS, ElasticNet, and DFM. Expanded the test suite from 5 to 19 tests. Tagged version v1.0. The combination turned out to be the best result of the entire project.
🏆 Combination V3 RMSE = 1.72% — the overall winner

🔬 The Models Explained Simply

Here's an intuitive comparison of all six approaches — what each one actually does, and an everyday analogy to make it concrete:

AR(p) — Autoregression
Uses only past GDP values to predict future GDP. Automatically picks how many past quarters to include. The benchmark that everything else must beat.
Predicting tomorrow's weather using only today's temperature — no other data allowed.
Bridge Equations
Each of the 12 indicators gets its own simple regression against GDP. The 12 individual forecasts are then averaged together. Handles ragged edge with partial quarterly averages.
12 expert analysts each read one indicator and give a GDP estimate. Take the average of all 12 opinions.
MIDAS Regression
Like bridge, but keeps the three monthly observations within a quarter as separate features rather than averaging them. Lets the model weight months differently.
Same 12 experts, but each one can split their opinion across early, middle, and late month observations — more granular signal.
Lasso / ElasticNet
All 12 indicators × 3 monthly lags = 37 features in one model. Regularization automatically zeros out unimportant features, preventing overfitting. Trained with time-series cross-validation.
One expert reads all 37 data points at once but has a strict "ignore the noise" rule that forces them to focus on only the most informative signals.
Dynamic Factor Model
Assumes a hidden "economic health" factor drives all 12 indicators simultaneously. Uses the Kalman filter to estimate this factor and extract a GDP nowcast. Handles missing data natively.
A doctor diagnosing overall health from 12 vital signs — not reading each one separately, but looking for the underlying condition that explains all of them together.
🏆 Method Combination
Simple equal-weight average of the Bridge, MIDAS, ElasticNet, and DFM forecasts. No single model dominates. Biases from different models partially cancel out, and prediction variance is reduced through diversification.
A committee vote where every method family gets one equal vote. No single approach can dominate, and errors tend to offset each other.

What the Numbers Actually Mean

A typical pre-COVID quarter grew at about 2–3%. The AR model's forecast error was ±2.47 percentage points. The best model — the combination at vintage 3 — brought that down to ±1.72 percentage points: a 30% reduction in forecast error.

✅ Concrete Example

If GDP is going to grow at exactly 2.5%, the AR model's forecast could easily land anywhere from 0% to 5%. The combination model narrows that to roughly 0.8% to 4.2% — a meaningfully tighter interval for decision-makers.

The central nowcasting result is confirmed: forecasts get more accurate as the quarter fills in. Here's the Bridge combination's improvement across vintages:

The tiny Month 2 → Month 3 improvement is a key insight: at the end of Month 3 (quarter-end), most monthly data is still missing because publication lags extend into the following month. "End of month 3" is informationally much closer to "end of month 2" than you'd expect.

Why the DFM Underperforms

⚠️ The DFM Result: 2.77% — Worse than the AR Baseline

The DFM was expected to be the flagship model. Central banks use it everywhere. But with only 12 indicators and the publication-lag constraint, it can't show its potential. Two reasons:

  • Too few indicators. Central banks run DFMs with 50–200 series. With 12, there's not enough diversity of signal for the latent factor structure to add value over simpler regression.
  • The publication lag trap. At quarter-end, most monthly data is still NaN. The Kalman filter has nothing to update with, so it essentially uses the same information as Month 2. No practical advantage over simpler models.

Why the Combination Wins

The method combination (1.72% pre-COVID RMSE) beats every individual model. This happens because of bias cancellation:

  • Bridge and MIDAS consistently under-predict GDP growth (negative bias)
  • The DFM consistently over-predicts (positive bias, ~+2.1pp)
  • When averaged, these biases partially offset each other
  • The combination also reduces prediction variance through diversification — the same reason a financial portfolio is less risky than any single stock
⚠️ What Didn't Work

Nothing is significant at the 5% level — the conventional gold standard. With only 60 quarterly observations in the pre-COVID sample, confidence intervals are wide. This is a fundamental constraint of quarterly macroeconomic evaluation, not a failure of the methods. Significance at the 10% level is the realistic bar here.

🔎 Key Limitations

Good research is honest about what it can and cannot claim. Here are the four most important caveats:

⚠️ Revised Data, Not Real-Time Data

The FRED data used here is the current, most recently revised vintage. A forecaster working in 2005 would have seen noisier, less reliable data — revisions hadn't happened yet. This means our accuracy figures are probably slightly optimistic. A fully rigorous study would use ALFRED (Archival FRED) to get the exact data available on each historical date.

⚠️ Approximate Publication Lags

The publication delays in our model are fixed constants (e.g., "payrolls = 5 days after month-end"). In reality, release dates shift by a few days each month, and occasional delays occur. A production nowcasting system would track the actual release calendar in real time.

⚠️ No Comparison Against Professionals

The NY Fed publishes a weekly nowcast. The Survey of Professional Forecasters publishes quarterly consensus estimates. We don't know whether our methods add value beyond what professional forecasters already produce with much larger datasets and more sophisticated systems.

⚠️ Small Panel for the DFM

A production dynamic factor model at a central bank would use 100+ series. Our DFM with 12 indicators reflects a small-panel limitation, not the fundamental potential of the approach. With FRED-MD's ~120 series, the DFM would likely perform much better.

🎯 The Big Takeaway

✅ The Answer to the Hypothesis

Yes — monthly economic data does help predict GDP before the official estimate arrives. But the improvement is modest, and mainly materialises in the middle of the quarter once the most informative series (payrolls, industrial production) have been released.

The best result — a 30% reduction in forecast error relative to a pure time-series baseline — is economically meaningful but statistically fragile with only 60 quarterly observations. The combination of methods adds genuine value through bias diversification.

Surprisingly, the simple methods (bridge equations, MIDAS) perform as well as or better than the more sophisticated approaches (Lasso, ElasticNet, DFM) on this dataset size. Complexity doesn't always win.
