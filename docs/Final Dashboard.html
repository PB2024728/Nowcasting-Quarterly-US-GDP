💡 The Hypothesis & Motivation

Every three months, the U.S. government publishes an official estimate of how fast the economy grew. This number — GDP growth — is one of the most watched economic statistics in the world. The problem: it arrives about six weeks after the quarter ends, and the first estimate is subject to revision for years.

🎯 The Central Question

Monthly economic indicators — factory output, job numbers, retail sales, stock prices — stream in throughout the quarter as it happens. Can we use these early signals to predict GDP before the official estimate is released? And if so, how much better are these predictions compared to simply extrapolating past GDP?

This is called "nowcasting" — a word combining "now" and "forecasting." Central banks (the Fed, ECB, Bank of England) use nowcasting to monitor the economy in real time before official statistics are published. This project rigorously tests five different nowcasting approaches on 20 years of data.

⚡ Why This Is Hard

Four concrete challenges had to be solved explicitly:

1

Mixed Frequencies

GDP is quarterly. Monthly indicators arrive throughout the quarter. A forecast made in early January uses different data than one in late March, both predicting the same Q1 number.

2

Publication Delays

Data doesn't arrive the moment a month ends. Payrolls come out ~5 days later. Factory output ~17 days. Durable goods ~28 days. We simulate exactly what a real forecaster would have seen on any given date.

3

Too Few Data Points

Only ~60 normal (pre-COVID) quarters to evaluate on. Statistical tests have limited power with this few observations — hard to prove one method is definitively better.

4

The COVID Outlier

2020Q2 GDP collapsed −31.6% annualized. No model trained on normal data could predict a pandemic. Results are reported three ways: full sample, pre-COVID, and COVID-excluded.

⚠️ The "Ragged Edge" Problem

At any point in time, data looks like a table with ragged right edges — recent months are only partially filled in. Every model was evaluated using only data available on the actual forecast date — never anything from the future.

📊 The Data

All data is from FRED (Federal Reserve Economic Data), a free public database. The target is real GDP growth: 400 × log(GDP_t / GDP_{t-1}) — annualized quarterly percent change.

🔬 The Models Explained Simply

AR(p) — Autoregression
Uses only past GDP to predict future GDP. Automatically picks how many past quarters to include. The benchmark.
Predicting tomorrow's weather using only today's temperature — no other data allowed.
Bridge Equations
Each of the 12 indicators gets its own simple regression against GDP. The 12 individual forecasts are averaged together.
12 expert analysts each read one indicator and give a GDP estimate. Take the average of all 12 opinions.
MIDAS Regression
Like bridge, but keeps monthly observations separate rather than averaging them. Lets the model weight months differently.
Same 12 experts, but each one can split their view into early, middle, and late-month observations.
Lasso / ElasticNet
All 12 indicators × 3 monthly lags = 37 features in one model. Regularization auto-zeros unimportant features. Time-series cross-validation.
One expert reads all 37 data points but has a strict rule: ignore everything that doesn't genuinely help.
Dynamic Factor Model
Assumes a hidden "economic health" factor drives all 12 indicators. Kalman filter estimates this factor and extracts a GDP nowcast.
A doctor diagnosing overall health from 12 vital signs — looking for the underlying condition, not reading each sign separately.
🏆 Method Combination
Simple equal-weight average of Bridge, MIDAS, ElasticNet, and DFM. No single model dominates. Biases from different models partially cancel out.
Committee vote — every method family gets one equal vote, and errors offset each other.

✅ What the 30% Improvement Means in Practice

If GDP is going to grow at 2.5%, the AR model's forecast could land anywhere from 0% to 5%. The combination model narrows that to roughly 0.8% to 4.2% — a meaningfully tighter interval for decision-makers.

Key Chart Insights

⚠️ Why the DFM Underperforms

The DFM (purple line, Chart 2) barely improves from Month 2 to Month 3 — and actually sits above the AR baseline throughout. The reason: at quarter-end, most month-3 data still hasn't been published (publication lags extend into the following month). The Kalman filter has little new information to work with, so V3 ≈ V2 informationally. A DFM needs 50–200 indicators to show its full potential.

✅ Why the Combination (orange, Chart 2) Wins

The regression models (Bridge, MIDAS) consistently under-predict GDP. The DFM consistently over-predicts. When averaged, these biases cancel out. The combination also reduces variance through diversification — the same logic as a balanced investment portfolio.

🔎 Key Limitations

⚠️ Revised Data, Not Real-Time Data

The FRED data used is the current, most recently revised vintage. A forecaster in 2005 would have seen noisier data — revisions hadn't happened yet. Our accuracy figures are probably slightly optimistic. A fully rigorous study would use ALFRED (Archival FRED) for exact historical vintages.

⚠️ Approximate Publication Lags

Publication delays are fixed constants (e.g., "payrolls = 5 days after month-end"). In reality they shift by a few days each month. A production system would track the actual release calendar in real time.

⚠️ No Professional Forecaster Comparison

The NY Fed publishes a weekly nowcast. The Survey of Professional Forecasters publishes quarterly consensus estimates. We don't know if our methods add value beyond what professional forecasters already produce with far larger datasets.

⚠️ Small Panel for the DFM

A production DFM at a central bank would use 100+ series. With 12 indicators, the DFM can't show its full potential. With FRED-MD's ~120 series, results would likely be much better.

🎯 The Big Takeaway

✅ The Answer

Yes — monthly economic data does help predict GDP before the official estimate arrives. But the improvement is modest, and mainly materialises in the middle of the quarter once payrolls and industrial production have been released.

The best result — a 30% reduction in forecast error — is economically meaningful but statistically fragile with only 60 quarterly observations. The combination of methods adds genuine value through bias diversification.

Surprisingly, simple methods (Bridge, MIDAS) match or beat more sophisticated ones (Lasso, DFM) at this dataset size. Complexity doesn't always win.
